Mount Washington, New Hampshire

Tuckerman Ravine

A history carved in ice and snow

  • 20,000+ years ago

    The Laurentide Ice Sheet

    The Laurentide Ice Sheet blanketed much of North America, including present-day New Hampshire, slowly carving out the landscape. As the glacier retreated, it left behind the steep headwalls and bowl-shaped terrain that define the ravine today.

  • 1914

    First skier in the ravine

    John S. Apperson became the first person to ski in Tuckerman Ravine, pioneering what would become one of the most celebrated ski destinations in the Northeast.

  • 1920s – Early 1930s

    Early ski pioneers

    Members of the Appalachian Mountain Club and Dartmouth Outing Club begin regularly exploring and skiing the ravine, establishing the traditions of spring skiing that endure to this day.

  • 1931

    First headwall descent

    John Charleton and Charley Proctor completed the first descent of the 50-degree headwall — a landmark moment in American skiing history.

  • 1931 — one week later

    Summit-to-headwall descent

    Harvard skiers Robert Livermore, Brad Trafford, and Robert Balch completed the first summit-to-headwall descent, later going on to help found the Hochgebirge Ski Club.

  • First inferno

    April 16, 1933

    The American Inferno race

    The first American Inferno ski race was held, starting at the summit of Mount Washington and ending at Pinkham Notch. Organized by the Ski Club Hochgebirge, Hollis Phillips won with a time of 14:41.3.

  • 1936

    First woman on the headwall

    Mary Bird is believed to be the first woman to ski the headwall, a milestone in the mountain's inclusive skiing history.

  • Record unbroken

    1939

    Toni Matt's legendary run

    During the 1939 Inferno, Toni Matt set a record that stands to this day — descending from the summit of Mount Washington to Pinkham Notch in 6 minutes 29.2 seconds, reaching speeds up to 90 mph as he straightlined down the headwall.

  • 1939

    Mount Washington Volunteer Ski Patrol founded

    Henry "Swampy" Paris founded the Mount Washington Volunteer Ski Patrol (MWVSP) as the "Mount Washington Safety Patrol," supporting the U.S. Forest Service with rescue and first aid for spring skiers out of Hermit Lake.

  • 1940s

    Brooks Dodge pioneers the lines

    Brooks Dodge pioneers many iconic routes — Dodge's Drop, Sluice, Icefall, and Duchess — helping define the vocabulary of early extreme skiing in the ravine.

  • Loss

    1951

    Ravine shelter burns

    The original Tuckerman Ravine Shelter burned down due to a worker's carelessness, a significant loss for the mountain community.

  • 1950s & 60s

    Metal skis and growing popularity

    Metal skis transform the sport and spring skiing at Tuckerman becomes a major Northeast tradition. Avalanches and icefalls posed serious dangers to growing crowds; the Forest Service ultimately shifted focus toward visitor education and safer backcountry decision-making.

  • 1980s

    Preservation and telemark rise

    Proposals to mechanize access via snowcats, helicopters, and the Cog Railway were defeated by environmentalists and the Forest Service, preserving the hike-in tradition. Telemark skiing gained popularity for its efficiency on the mountain's terrain.

  • 1990s

    Snowboarding and winter mountaineering

    Snowboarding brought a younger crowd and grew quickly in the ravine. Winter mountaineering also increased, with year-round exploration expanding the mountain's use — though Mount Washington's extreme weather continued to pose serious risks.

  • Present day

    2000s – Today

    A bucket-list destination

    Tuckerman Ravine has become a bucket-list destination for skiers, snowboarders, and spectators alike. Backcountry skiing's growing popularity has expanded visitation beyond the traditional spring season, reinforcing the enduring importance of education, preparation, and responsible decision-making in a high-risk environment.
